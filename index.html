About Me

I'm computer architecture researcher interested in building diverse, specialized architectures, and the addressing the usability challenges they present. I believe in problems first research, and as such I work at any level of the computing stack necessary to achieve this. I'm currently pursuing my PhD at UC Santa Barbara, co-advised by Tim Sherwood and Jonathan Balkind. Before coming to Santa Barbara I did my undergraduate in Computer Science and Computer Engineering at UCSC, where I worked with professors Heiner Litz and Tyler Sorensen on CPU architecture and GPU programming model specifications, respectively.


In 2025 I received the Dean’s, Chancellor’s, and Steck Family awards for my Senior thesis, Incorrect Path Prediction for Power Throttling Speculative Execution. The Steck Family Award is given to the single best undergraduate thesis at UCSC each year. I am also a recipient of UCSB’s Computer Science department fellowship.

Past Research Projects

GPU Subgroup Semantics

Subgroups (i.e. warps in CUDA) are logical groups of threads in the GPU execution hierarchy. While it is informally recognized that they execute in lockstep, specification designers are hesitant to formally say this in the programming model, leaving the semantics of collective subgroup operations underdefined. This work aims to formalize several potential subgroup execution models and evaluate their support on existing systems.

CPU Frontend Microarchitecture

Datacenter CPUs have very deep and wide pipelines. They rely on branch predictions to overcome control hazards and keep the pipeline full. While modern branch predictors are highly accurate, bad speculation is still an issue, especially for larger applications. This project aims to predict when the frontend is producing erroneous instructions and leverage this prediction for a variety of efficiency and performance optimizations.
